01
Introduction to the Turbine Section
What the turbine does and why it matters
▼

Main Function

The turbine section is one of the most critical parts of a gas turbine engine. It extracts energy from hot exhaust gases and converts it into mechanical energy.

This mechanical energy is used to:

  • Drive the compressor
  • Power engine accessories
  • Drive fans, propellers, or shafts
💡 Key Concept
The turbine converts kinetic energy → mechanical energy.

Turbine Location

The turbine section is located directly behind the combustion chamber. Hot combustion gases flow from the combustion chamber, through the nozzle guide vanes, and across the turbine blades.

02
Purpose & Energy Absorption
How much energy the turbine absorbs and what drives its load
▼

Gas Generator Turbine

The gas generator turbine's primary role is to drive the compressor section.

It absorbs approximately 60%–70% of the exhaust gas energy.

Remaining Energy

The energy not used by the gas generator turbine may drive:

  • Fan systems
  • Propellers
  • Helicopter rotor shafts
  • Additional turbine stages

What Determines Turbine Load?

  • Compressor size and type
  • Number of accessories
  • Fan or propeller load

04
Axial Flow Turbine
Components, operation, and blade types
▼

Main Components

  • Turbine rotors (wheels)
  • Stationary vanes (nozzle guide vanes)

How It Works

Hot gases flow parallel to the engine axis, pass through nozzle guide vanes, strike the turbine blades, and cause turbine rotation.

Turbine Blade Types

1. Impulse Blades

Gas pressure drops mainly in the nozzle. The blade changes gas direction.

2. Reaction Blades

Pressure drops across both the nozzle and the rotor blades.

Nozzle Guide Vanes (NGV)

These stationary vanes:

  • Direct gases onto turbine blades at the correct angle
  • Increase gas velocity by forming small nozzle passages
  • Control gas angle and speed to improve turbine efficiency
💡 Key Concept
NGVs convert pressure energy into velocity — they accelerate the gas before it hits the blades.
05
Radial Inflow Turbine
Simple, strong, and used in smaller engines
▼

Characteristics

The radial inflow turbine is simple, strong, inexpensive, and easy to manufacture.

Operating Principle

Hot gases enter radially from the outside, strike the turbine wheel blades, and exit axially.

Applications

  • Small gas turbine engines
  • APUs (Auxiliary Power Units)
  • Small turboprop engines

Main Turbine Components
Stator and rotor sections explained
▼

Stator Section

Includes: Nozzle guide vanes, inlet guide vanes

Function: Directs gas flow and converts pressure into velocity.

Rotor Section

Includes: Turbine blades, turbine disk, turbine shaft

Function: Extracts energy from exhaust gases and rotates the shaft.

🧠 Memory Aid
Stator = Stationary = directs flow  |  Rotor = Rotates = extracts energy

Turbine Wheel

The turbine wheel consists of a turbine disk with attached turbine blades.

Turbine Disk

  • Supports the blades
  • Transfers power to the shaft

Turbine Shaft

  • Connects turbine to compressor
  • Transmits rotational energy
08
Forces Acting on Turbine Blades
Extreme temperature and centrifugal stress
▼

Operating Conditions

Turbine blades operate under:

  • Extremely high temperature
  • Very high rotational speed

Centrifugal Loads

High rotational speed creates severe centrifugal force on the blades.

Temperature Effects

High temperature weakens blade materials and increases stress on components.

⚠️ Safety Critical
Engine speed and temperature must remain within limits to prevent turbine failure and maintain safe operation.
09
Blade Attachment Methods
Fir-tree roots and retention techniques
▼

Fir-Tree Root Design

The most common and reliable blade attachment method.

Advantages:

  • Strong attachment
  • Handles thermal expansion well
  • Resists centrifugal loads
💡 Key Concept
The fir-tree root is the standard turbine blade attachment — it accommodates thermal growth and withstands centrifugal force.

Blade Retention Methods

Blades may be further secured using:

  • Peening
  • Welding
  • Lock tabs
  • Riveting
10
Shaft & Coupling Methods
How the shaft connects to the disk and compressor
▼

Shaft-to-Disk Connection

Welding

Shaft welded directly to disk.

Bolting ✔ Preferred

Shaft hub bolted to disk. Easier maintenance and replacement.

Compressor-to-Turbine Connection

The turbine shaft connects to the compressor using a splined coupling.

Purpose: Transfers rotational power and allows alignment between shafts.

11
Blade Materials & Creep
Critical concept: how heat causes permanent deformation
▼

Material Requirements

Turbine blades require materials that can withstand:

  • High temperatures
  • High stress
  • Creep

Creep — Definition

Creep is the slow, permanent deformation of material caused by prolonged heat and stress.

Consequences of creep:

  • Blade elongation
  • Distortion
  • Reduced efficiency
  • Blade failure
⚠️ Critical Principle
Creep increases as temperature increases.
💡 Exam Tip
Turbine blade creep is caused by prolonged exposure to high temperature and centrifugal stress.

All key points in one place
▼
⚡ Quick Recap
  • ✔ Turbine section is located directly behind the combustion chamber
  • ✔ Turbine drives the compressor and accessories
  • ✔ Gas generator turbine absorbs ~60%–70% of exhaust gas energy
  • ✔ Axial turbines — used in large engines, higher efficiency, high mass flow
  • ✔ Radial turbines — simpler, cheaper, used in small engines/APUs
  • ✔ Nozzle guide vanes direct and accelerate hot gases onto blades
  • ✔ Impulse blades: pressure drop in nozzle | Reaction blades: pressure drop in nozzle + rotor
  • ✔ Turbine blades face extreme temperature and centrifugal stress
  • ✔ Fir-tree roots are the most common blade attachment method
  • ✔ Bolting is preferred for shaft-to-disk connection (easier maintenance)
  • ✔ Splined coupling connects the turbine shaft to the compressor
  • ✔ Creep = slow permanent deformation from heat + stress; increases with temperature
