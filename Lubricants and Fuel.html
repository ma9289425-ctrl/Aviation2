Introduction to Turbine Engine Lubricants

Why turbine engines demand specialist oils

▾

Gas turbine engines operate under extreme conditions — high temperatures, high rotational speeds, and heavy bearing loads. These conditions demand oils that go far beyond what standard mineral oils can offer.

🌡️

High Temperatures

Engine components reach temperatures that would cause conventional oils to break down rapidly.

⚙️

High Rotational Speeds

Bearings spin at very high RPM, requiring oil that flows freely under all conditions.

⚖️

Heavy Bearing Loads

Despite using antifriction bearings (ball & roller), load-carrying ability is still critical.

💡 Key Concept

Turbine engines use primarily ball bearings and roller bearings (antifriction bearings). This allows the use of lower viscosity oils compared to reciprocating engines — but the oil must still be specially formulated synthetic oil.

⚗️

Required Oil Properties

Six critical characteristics turbine oil must possess

▾
💧
Proper Viscosity
Thick enough for load-carrying, thin enough for free flow and easy lubrication
🚫
Low Volatility
Resists evaporation at high temperatures and high altitudes
🫧
Anti-Foaming
Foam reduces lubrication effectiveness — oil must resist foam formation
🔩
Seal Compatibility
Must not damage natural rubber or synthetic seals
🧹
Low Carbon Formation
Minimises carbon deposits, varnish, and lacquer buildup
💡 Viscosity Dual Requirement

There is an inherent tension in oil viscosity requirements:

RequirementPurpose
High viscosityLoad-carrying ability
Low viscosityGood flow and lubrication at all temperatures
⚠️ Why These Properties Matter

Failure to meet any of these requirements risks bearing damage, seal degradation, carbon buildup, or oil starvation — any of which can lead to engine failure.

🧪

Synthetic Turbine Oils

Why synthetic is the only choice for modern turbines

▾

Modern turbine engines exclusively use synthetic oils, which are specially developed for high-temperature turbine operation. They significantly outperform mineral-based oils in turbine environments.

🧱

Reduced Deposit Formation

Produce less coke and lacquer compared to mineral oils, keeping the engine cleaner.

🛡️

High-Temperature Performance

Resist thermal breakdown and evaporation even at extreme operating temperatures.

📝 Exam Tip

Synthetic turbine oils are preferred because they resist high-temperature breakdown and reduce deposit formation — two key advantages over mineral oils.

📋

Type II Oil Characteristics

The standard classification and its key properties

▾
💡 Type II Classification

Most turbine oils are classified as Type II turbine oils. These contain additives providing: thermal protection, oxidation resistance, load-carrying ability, and low-temperature flow.

💨
Vapour Phase Deposit Resistance
Minimises carbon deposits that form from oil vapours in hot zones
🏋️
Load Carrying Ability
Provides adequate lubrication under heavy bearing loads
✨
Cleanliness
Reduces sludge formation and internal contamination
🧱
Bulk Stability
Resists physical change and chemical breakdown, especially from oxidation
🤝
Compatibility
Same-spec oils can technically mix, though mixing brands is discouraged
🔒
Seal Wear Protection
Protects carbon seals and seal contact surfaces from wear
⚠️ Signs of Oil Oxidation

Oxidised oil may show increased viscosity and increased acidity — both indicators that oil condition is degrading and a change may be necessary.

🧠 Memory Aid — Type II Properties

V-L-C-B-C-S → Vapour resistance · Load carrying · Cleanliness · Bulk stability · Compatibility · Seal protection

Spectrometric Oil Analysis Programme (SOAP)

Early detection of engine wear through oil sampling

▾
💡 What is SOAP?

SOAP is a condition monitoring programme that detects early engine wear by analysing metallic particles suspended in oil samples. Results are measured in Parts Per Million (PPM).

⚙️
Engine Operates
Tiny metal particles enter the oil

🛢️

🧫

Oil sample extracted for analysis
→
📊

Metallic elements & contaminants measured
→
🔔
Trend Compared
Current vs. previous samples reviewed
  • An increase in certain metals may indicate bearing wear, gear wear, or component failure
  • Maintenance personnel compare current metal levels with previous samples — trend analysis is key
  • A sudden spike in wear metals may trigger inspection or component replacement
⚡ SOAP Benefits
  • Early fault detection before failure occurs
  • Prevents major engine failures
  • Improves maintenance planning
  • Increases overall engine reliability

Turbine Engine Fuels — Overview

Characteristics of jet fuel and how it differs from AVGAS

▾
⚠️ Critical Safety Warning

Jet fuel must NEVER be mixed with AVGAS or used in reciprocating aircraft engines. Incorrect fuelling can cause engine damage or engine failure.

Turbine engines use jet fuel — a hydrocarbon-based fuel similar to kerosene. It is fundamentally different from AVGAS (used in piston engines) in its physical and chemical properties.

Property Jet Fuel AVGAS
Volatility Lower Higher
Boiling Point Higher Lower
Viscosity Higher Lower
Hydrocarbon Molecules Larger (more carbon atoms) Smaller
💨

Fuel Volatility

The balancing act between evaporation and startability

▾
💡 Definition

Volatility = how easily a fuel evaporates. It affects engine starting, vapour lock resistance, and cold-weather operation.

🔒

Low Volatility Needed For:

✔ Reduced evaporation losses
✔ Reduced vapour lock risk

🚀

Higher Volatility Needed For:

✔ Easy engine starting
✔ Better cold-weather operation

Fuel TypeVapour Pressure
AVGAS~7 psi
Jet A~0.125 psi
Jet B~2–3 psi
📝 Exam Note

Jet A has very low volatility (low vapour pressure). Jet B has higher volatility than Jet A — this is why Jet B performs better in very cold climates (easier cold-weather starting).

🛢️

Types of Turbine Fuel

Jet A, Jet A-1, and Jet B — differences at a glance

▾
Jet A
Freezing Point: −40°C
  • Most common fuel in continental USA
  • Kerosene-based
  • Low volatility
Jet A-1
Freezing Point: −47°C
  • Most widely used fuel worldwide
  • Similar to Jet A
  • Better cold-weather capability
Jet B
Freezing Point: −50°C
  • Blend of kerosene and gasoline
  • Higher volatility
  • Used in Alaska & Canada
Fuel TypeFreezing PointMain FeaturePrimary Use
Jet A −40°C Common US fuel, kerosene-based Continental USA
Jet A-1 −47°C Better low-temperature performance Worldwide standard
Jet B −50°C Kerosene/gasoline blend, higher volatility Very cold climates (Alaska, Canada)
🧠 Memory Aid — Freezing Points

Cold gets colder as you go: Jet A (−40°C) → Jet A-1 (−47°C) → Jet B (−50°C). Jet B is the most cold-weather capable.

Fuel Additives

What additives are used and why

▾
Problem PreventedAdditive Type
Corrosion Corrosion inhibitors
Fuel oxidation Oxidation inhibitors
Ice formation Anti-icing additives
Bacteria / microbial growth Biocides
💡 Why Additives Matter

Without additives, jet fuel is susceptible to corrosion, degradation, ice crystal formation (which can block fuel filters), and microbial contamination in fuel tanks — all of which are safety-critical concerns.

🏷️

Fuel Identification

How to distinguish fuels and why it is safety-critical

▾
⚠️ Safety Rule

Never release an aircraft for flight with unknown fuel onboard. Using the wrong fuel can damage engines, cause engine failure, and create major safety hazards.

  • Jet fuel appearance: Colourless or straw-coloured — no dyes added
  • AVGAS appearance: Contains coloured dyes — helps distinguish it from jet fuel
  • Jet fuel nozzles: Larger than AVGAS fillers — a physical design feature to help prevent misfuelling
  • Fuel systems: Use colour-coded labels, markings, and fuel cap identifiers
🧠 Quick Rule

Jet fuel = clear or straw. AVGAS = coloured (dyed). When in doubt — do not fuel.

  • Turbine engines use ball and roller (antifriction) bearings → allows lower viscosity oil

  • Jet fuel nozzles are larger than AVGAS fillers (misfuelling prevention)

  • Never use jet fuel in reciprocating engines
  • Never release an aircraft with unknown fuel onboard
