Introduction to Turbine Fuel Systems
Purpose, power control, and operating conditions
CORE ▼

The fuel system is one of the most important and complex systems in a gas turbine engine. Its primary role is to supply the right amount of fuel at the right pressure to ensure efficient combustion under all conditions.

⛽
Correct Quantity
Fuel must be delivered in precise amounts for each phase of flight and engine operation.
🔀
Correct Pressure
Fuel must be supplied at the correct pressure to ensure proper nozzle atomization and combustion.
🔥
Efficient Combustion
The system must maintain efficient combustion through all operating conditions.

Power Control

💡 Key Concept — Engine Power
In turbine engines, engine power is controlled primarily by varying fuel flow to the combustion chamber. In turboprop engines, thrust also depends on propeller blade angle in addition to fuel flow.

Operating Conditions

  • Engine start
  • Acceleration
  • Cruise
  • Deceleration
  • High altitude flight
📋
Fuel System Requirements
What the fuel system must achieve at all times
CORE ▼

The fuel system must meet multiple performance requirements to ensure safe and reliable engine operation across all flight phases.

📊
Correct Quantity & Atomization
Deliver exact fuel quantity and ensure fine spray for efficient ignition and combustion.
🔄
Stable Transitions
Support smooth acceleration and deceleration without combustion instability.
🎯
Automatic Adjustment
Automatically compensate for changes in ambient temperature, pressure, and air density.
⚡ Quick Recap — Automatic Compensation
  • Ambient temperature changes
  • Pressure changes
  • Air density changes

Fuel/Air Ratio Problems
Rich blowout and lean flameout — critical failure modes
SAFETY ▼

The fuel-air mixture must remain within safe operating limits at all times. Both over-rich and over-lean conditions lead to dangerous combustion failures.

🔴 Rich Blowout
  • Too much fuel supplied
  • Insufficient oxygen available
  • Combustion temperature falls
  • Engine instability / flame failure
🟡 Lean Flameout
  • Too little fuel supplied
  • Fuel quantity too low for combustion
  • Flame is extinguished
  • Engine flame-out results
⚠️
Danger — Rich Blowout Effects

A rich mixture can cause: compressor stall, turbine overheating, and flame instability.

💡 Key Concept
Proper combustion requires a correct fuel-to-air ratio. Both extremes — too rich or too lean — cause combustion failure and potential engine damage.
⚡
Acceleration Control
Why fuel must increase gradually during acceleration
SAFETY ▼

During acceleration, the engine needs extra fuel to increase turbine energy. However, the rate of fuel increase must be carefully controlled.

✅ Gradual Increase
  • Fuel-air ratio stays balanced
  • Smooth combustion maintained
  • Compressor remains stable
  • Engine accelerates safely
❌ Rapid Increase
  • Over-rich mixture forms
  • Compressor stall may occur
  • Rich blowout is possible
  • Engine instability results
⚡
Exam Tip

Rapid fuel increase during acceleration can cause compressor stall or rich blowout.

Fuel Atomization
Why fine spray matters for combustion efficiency
CORE ▼

The fuel system must deliver fuel as a finely atomized spray. The quality of atomization directly affects combustion efficiency, ignition reliability, and overall engine performance.

Why Atomization Matters

  • Improves combustion quality
  • Improves fuel-air mixing
  • Aids reliable ignition
  • Increases combustion efficiency
💡 Fuel Nozzle Role
Fuel nozzles spray fuel into the combustion chamber and produce the required spray pattern. Their design is critical to efficient and stable combustion.
🔑
Engine Start Requirement

During engine start, fuel must enter the combustion chamber in a combustible condition to allow reliable ignition and stable flame establishment.

🔑
Engine Starting Requirements
Conditions needed for reliable ignition and stable combustion
CORE ▼

Engine start places specific demands on the fuel system. Fuel must be delivered in a form that allows immediate ignition and sustained combustion.

  • Combustible condition: Fuel must enter the combustion chamber ready to ignite.
  • Easy ignition: Atomization must be sufficient for reliable spark ignition.
  • Stable combustion: Flame must be sustained after ignition without instability.
⚡ Quick Recap
  • Correct atomization is essential during start
  • Fuel quantity at start must be carefully controlled
  • Too little → lean flameout; Too much → rich blowout

FADEC Fuel Control System
Full Authority Digital Electronic Control — architecture and role
FADEC ▼

FADEC (Full Authority Digital Electronic Control) is a fully electronic fuel control system that manages fuel flow and engine operation with no hydromechanical backup.

🧠 Memory Aid
FADEC
Full  Authority  Digital  Electronic  Control

FADEC Controls

  • Fuel flow to combustion chamber
  • Overall engine operation
  • Engine performance parameters
⚠️
Critical Distinction

A true FADEC system has NO hydromechanical backup fuel control. The system is fully authority — it has complete control over engine fuel.

🖥️
EEC & FADEC Components
Electronic Engine Control, metering valve, sensors, and power supply
FADEC ▼

The EEC (Electronic Engine Control) acts as the engine's computer, processing sensor data and commanding the fuel metering valve to deliver the correct fuel flow.

ComponentFunction
EEC (Electronic Engine Control)Computes fuel requirements; the engine computer
Fuel Metering ValveAdjusts fuel flow according to EEC commands

EEC Functions

  • Receives data from engine sensors and aircraft systems
  • Calculates required fuel flow
  • Controls engine operation
  • Maintains safe operating limits

Pressure Inputs Monitored by the EEC

  • Ambient pressure
  • Burner pressure
  • LPC (Low Pressure Compressor) exit pressure
  • Fan inlet pressure

EEC Power Supply

⚡ Aircraft Electrical System
  • Primary power source
  • Powers EEC on ground
🔋 PMA (Permanent Magnet Alternator)
  • Powers EEC when engine is running
  • Independent of aircraft electrical bus
💡
Key Point — PMA

When the engine is running, the Permanent Magnet Alternator (PMA) powers the EEC directly, providing reliable operation independent of the aircraft electrical system.

🔁
Dual-Channel EEC Design
Redundancy, channel identification, and the programming plug
FADEC ▼

Modern FADEC systems use two independent electronic channels to ensure reliability and provide backup capability in the event of a channel failure.

Advantages of Dual-Channel Design

  • Increased reliability
  • Backup capability if one channel fails
  • Improved safety

Fuel Heaters
Ice prevention in the fuel system at high altitude
SAFETY ▼

At high altitude, fuel temperature can fall below 0°C. Any residual water in the fuel may freeze, forming ice crystals that block fuel filters and restrict fuel flow.

🧊
Ice in Fuel — Danger

Ice crystals can block fuel filters, restrict fuel flow, and cause engine fuel starvation.

Fuel Heater Purpose

  • Prevent ice formation in the fuel
  • Melt existing ice crystals
  • Improve combustion quality

Types of Fuel Heaters

Heater TypeHeat Source
Air-to-liquid heat exchangerEngine bleed air
Liquid-to-liquid heat exchangerEngine oil

Warning System

  • Pressure drop warning switches detect filter blockage caused by ice.
  • If ice blocks the filter, a warning light illuminates in the cockpit.

Operation Modes

🖐 Manual
  • Pilot selects fuel heating
  • Activated based on conditions
🤖 Automatic (FADEC)
  • FADEC regulates fuel temperature
  • No pilot action required

Fuel Nozzle Types
Simplex, duplex, and airblast — comparison and characteristics
NOZZLES ▼

Fuel nozzles spray fuel into the combustion chamber. Nozzle design directly affects atomization quality, combustion efficiency, and engine reliability.

🔵
Simplex Nozzle
Earliest Design
  • Single fuel manifold
  • Single fuel passage
  • Components: nozzle tip, insert, fine mesh strainer
  • Poorer atomization at low engine speeds
🟢
Duplex Nozzle
Most Common
  • Dual fuel passages
  • Requires dual fuel manifold + flow divider / pressurizing valve
  • Better starting performance
  • Improved idle combustion
  • Better spray pattern across wide pressure range
🔵
Airblast Nozzle
Airflow-Assisted
  • Uses airflow for atomization (squirrel vanes)
  • Lower operating pressure required
  • Better combustion efficiency
  • Reduced local rich fuel areas
  • Reduced carbon buildup ("carboning")
✅
Airblast Nozzle Advantage

Airblast nozzles help prevent disturbed spray patterns caused by carbon deposits, reducing carboning and improving long-term combustion quality.

Nozzle Matching Requirement

⚠️
Critical Maintenance Point

All fuel nozzles in the same engine must deliver equal fuel flow. Uneven fuel flow causes uneven combustion, temperature imbalance, and reduced efficiency.

🔧
Nozzle Installation Methods
External vs. internal mounting and maintenance implications
NOZZLES ▼

Fuel nozzles can be mounted in two positions, each with different maintenance implications.

🔵 External Mounting
  • Nozzle attaches outside the combustion case
  • Easier maintenance access
🟡 Internal Mounting
  • Nozzle installs inside the combustion liner dome
  • Requires chamber cover removal for maintenance
