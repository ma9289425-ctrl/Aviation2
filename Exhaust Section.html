Introduction to the Exhaust Section

▼

Understanding the final section of a gas turbine engine and its role in thrust production.

💡
Key Definition

The exhaust section is the final part of a gas turbine engine, located directly behind the turbine section.

📍 Location

  • Directly behind the turbine section
  • At the rear of the engine

🎯 Main Functions

  • Directs hot gases rearward
  • Increases exhaust gas velocity
  • Produces thrust efficiently
  • Reduces turbulence in gas flow

⚡ Quick Recap

  • Exhaust section = final engine section
  • Converts hot gases into high-velocity flow
  • Output = propulsive force (thrust)
2

Main Components Overview

▼

The three major components that make up the exhaust section.

Component Function
Exhaust Cone Collects and smooths gas flow
Tailpipe Directs gases rearward
Exhaust Nozzle Accelerates gases to produce thrust
🧠
Memory Aid: C-T-N

Cone → Tailpipe → Nozzle (Gas flow sequence)

3

Exhaust Cone

▼

How the exhaust cone converts turbulent turbine output into smooth, efficient flow.

🎯 Main Functions

  • Collects gases leaving the turbine
  • Converts swirling gases into smooth flow

⚙️ How It Works

  • Slightly decreases gas velocity
  • Increases gas pressure
  • Uses a divergent passage
💡
Divergent Flow Principle

In a divergent (increasing area) passage: Velocity ↓  |  Pressure ↑

Property Effect in Divergent Passage
Velocity Decreases
Pressure Increases

▼

Detailed breakdown of exhaust cone assembly components.

🔧 Main Parts

  • Outer duct (outer shell)
  • Inner cone
  • Radial struts (support fins)
  • Tie rods (some designs)

📡 Outer Duct Features

  • Collects exhaust gases
  • Directs gases to exhaust nozzle
  • May contain thermocouple bosses
  • May have tie rod insertion points
🌡️
Thermocouples

Purpose: Measure Exhaust Gas Temperature (EGT)

5

Radial Struts

▼

Critical components that perform two essential functions in exhaust gas management.

1️⃣ Support Function

Hold the inner cone in position inside the exhaust duct

2️⃣ Flow Straightening

Reduce swirling airflow from the turbine

⚠️
Why Flow Straightening Matters

Without straightening: gases exit at an angle → turbulence increases → efficiency decreases

💡
Key Concept

Struts align gases into smooth axial flow for efficient thrust production.

6

Inner Cone

▼

The inner cone's role in turbulence reduction and cooling.

🎯 Function

  • Reduces turbulence behind turbine wheel
  • Smooths gas flow into exhaust nozzle

❄️ Cooling System

Some inner cones include a cooling hole at the rear tip

🔄
Cooling Air Flow Process

1. Air enters through rear opening → 2. Flows inside cone → 3. Cools turbine wheel area → 4. Returns to main gas flow

🔥
Why Cooling Is Necessary

Turbine operates at extremely high temperatures. Cooling prevents overheating and material damage.

7

Tailpipe

▼

The tailpipe structure and its flexible design features.

🎯 Function

Carries exhaust gases rearward

🔧 Construction Feature

Many tailpipes include bellows sections

💡
Purpose of Bellows
  • Allow thermal expansion
  • Enable easier installation
  • Reduce stress and warping

Exhaust System Insulation

▼

Protection methods for intense heat radiation from the exhaust section.

⚠️
Why Insulation Is Required

Without protection, intense heat radiation could damage nearby aircraft structure.

1️⃣ Insulation Blankets

Materials:

  • Aluminum foil layers
  • Fiberglass insulation

2️⃣ Shrouds

Additional protective coverings around exhaust components

⚡ Insulation Purposes

  • Protect aircraft structure (reduce heat transfer to airframe)
  • Improve engine performance (reduce heat loss)

Exhaust Nozzles

▼

Nozzle types and their applications for different exhaust velocities.

💡
Main Function

The exhaust nozzle accelerates exhaust gases to produce thrust.

Nozzle Type Application
Convergent Nozzle Subsonic exhaust flow
Convergent-Divergent Nozzle Supersonic exhaust flow

◀️ Convergent Nozzle

  • Nozzle area decreases
  • Gas velocity increases
  • Used when exhaust remains subsonic

◀️▶️ Convergent-Divergent Nozzle

  • First converges, then diverges
  • Accelerates gases to supersonic speed
🧠
Memory Aid

Convergent = Sub-sonic | Con-Di = Super-sonic

10

Fixed vs Variable Area Nozzles

▼

Understanding when and why variable geometry nozzles are required.

📐 Fixed Area Nozzle

  • Simple design
  • No moving parts

🔄 Variable Area Nozzle

Used mainly with afterburner (augmenter) systems

⚠️
Critical: Correct Nozzle Area
  • Too large → Thrust is wasted
  • Too small → Engine may choke or stall
💡
Why Variable Area Is Needed

Afterburner ON → Exhaust gas volume increases → Nozzle must open wider
Afterburner OFF → Nozzle closes to smaller area

📝 Exam Tip

  • Variable area nozzles are necessary in afterburning engines due to increased exhaust gas mass flow.
11

Thrust Reversers

▼

Systems that help slow aircraft after landing using redirected engine thrust.

💡
Purpose

Help slow the aircraft after landing. Modern aircraft are heavier and land at higher speeds — brakes alone may not provide enough stopping force.

Type Method
Mechanical Blockage Blocks and redirects exhaust gases
Aerodynamic Blockage Redirects fan airflow

Mechanical Blockage Reverser

⚙️ Operating Principle

Movable doors or clamshells block exhaust flow and redirect gases forward

🔧 Designs & Applications

  • Clamshell doors
  • Inverted cone systems
  • Usually on ducted turbofan engines
✅
Important Feature

When not in use, doors retract smoothly into nacelle structure.

Aerodynamic Blockage Reverser

🔧 Main Components

  • Translating cowl
  • Blocker doors
  • Cascade vanes
🔄
How It Works

1. Translating cowl moves rearward → 2. Blocker doors close rearward flow → 3. Fan air redirects through cascade vanes → 4. Air is directed forward

⚠️
Why Fan Air Is Used

The fan produces approximately 80% of engine thrust. Redirecting fan air provides effective braking.

⚡ Quick Recap: Thrust Reversers

  • Mechanical: Blocks and redirects core exhaust
  • Aerodynamic: Redirects fan airflow (most thrust)
  • Result: Forward-directed airflow slows aircraft rapidly
12

Thrust Vectoring

▼

Directing engine thrust in different directions for enhanced maneuverability.

💡
Definition

Thrust vectoring is the ability to direct engine thrust in different directions.

🎯 Benefits

  • Improved aircraft maneuverability
  • Short/vertical takeoff capability
  • Enhanced combat performance

⚙️ How It Works

The exhaust nozzle moves up or down to redirect engine thrust. Some vectoring nozzles can move approximately 20° up or down.

✈️ Military Aircraft

Used for extreme maneuvering

🚁 VTOL Aircraft

Used for vertical lift and transition to horizontal flight

Key Concepts Summary

▼

📋 Complete Revision Checklist

  • Exhaust Section: Final section — directs and accelerates exhaust gases
  • Exhaust Cone: Straightens and smooths gas flow (divergent passage)
  • Radial Struts: Support inner cone + reduce gas swirl
  • Exhaust Nozzle: Converts gas energy into thrust
  • Convergent Nozzle: Subsonic flow
  • Convergent-Divergent: Supersonic flow
  • Variable Area Nozzle: Required for afterburner systems
  • Thrust Reversers: Slow aircraft after landing
  • Aerodynamic Reverser: Uses fan airflow (~80% of thrust)
  • Thrust Vectoring: Redirects thrust for maneuverability (~20° movement)
🧠
Quick Facts to Remember
  • Exhaust gases must flow smoothly rearward
  • Divergent = velocity ↓, pressure ↑
  • Bellows allow thermal expansion
  • Fan produces ~80% of thrust (turbofans)
  • Vectoring nozzles: ~20° movement
