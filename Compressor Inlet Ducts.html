Module 15.3 — Gas Turbine Engines

Compressor Inlet Ducts

The first section of a gas turbine engine — delivering smooth, efficient airflow to the compressor while managing speed, pressure, and turbulence.

01 Introduction & Purpose of the Inlet Duct ▾

Understanding what the inlet duct does and why it matters.

📖 Definition

The compressor inlet duct is the first section of a gas turbine engine. It receives ambient air and delivers it to the compressor face.

🎯 Main Functions

  • Deliver air smoothly to the compressor
  • Minimize energy losses in the airflow
  • Reduce turbulence and aerodynamic drag
  • Improve overall engine efficiency
💡 Key Concept

A well-designed inlet duct improves compressor efficiency, helps prevent compressor stall, and enhances overall aircraft performance.

⚡ Quick Recap
  • Inlet duct = first engine section
  • Purpose: smooth, low-turbulence airflow to compressor
  • Good design = better efficiency + stall prevention
02 Compressor Pressure Ratio ▾

How the inlet duct contributes to the engine's pressure ratio.

📖 Definition

Compressor Pressure Ratio (CPR) = Compressor Outlet Pressure ÷ Compressor Inlet Pressure

CPR = Compressor Outlet PressureCompressor Inlet Pressure
💡 Key Concept

A higher pressure ratio generally means better engine performance, higher thermal efficiency, and increased thrust.

⚡ Quick Recap
  • CPR = outlet pressure ÷ inlet pressure
  • Higher CPR → better performance & thrust
  • The inlet duct contributes to CPR by managing intake pressure
03 Factors Affecting Airflow Through the Engine ▾

Three main factors that determine how much air enters the engine.

1. Compressor Speed (RPM)

Higher RPM draws in more air through the inlet.

2. Aircraft Forward Speed

Faster aircraft speed increases ram air entering the inlet, boosting intake pressure.

3. Ambient Air Density

Air density changes with altitude and temperature.

  • Dense air (low altitude, cold) → increases mass airflow → improves performance
  • Thin air (high altitude, hot) → decreases mass airflow → reduces performance
🧠 Memory Aid — "SDA"

Speed (compressor RPM) · Density (ambient air) · Aircraft speed (forward velocity) — the three factors affecting engine airflow.

⚡ Quick Recap
  • Compressor RPM → more speed = more air
  • Forward speed → ram effect increases intake air
  • Air density → denser air = better performance
04 Smooth Airflow & Compressor Stall ▾

Why uniform airflow is critical and what happens when it fails.

💡 Key Concept

The compressor requires smooth, stable, non-turbulent airflow to operate correctly.

📖 Definition

A compressor stall occurs when airflow over the compressor blades slows down, stops, or reverses direction.

⚠️ Safety Critical — Effects of Compressor Stall
  • ⚠️ Loss of thrust
  • ⚠️ Loud bangs or vibrations
  • ⚠️ Increased turbine temperatures
  • ⚠️ Possible engine damage
🧠 Exam Tip

Smooth airflow into the compressor is critical for stable engine operation. This is a commonly tested concept.

⚡ Quick Recap
  • Compressor needs smooth, stable airflow
  • Stall = airflow slows, stops, or reverses
  • Effects: thrust loss, vibration, high temps, damage
05 Variable Geometry Inlet Ducts ▾

How inlet ducts change shape to manage airflow at different speeds.

📖 Definition

A variable geometry inlet changes its shape during flight to control the airflow entering the engine.

💡 Key Concept

Even when the aircraft flies at supersonic speed (Mach > 1), the airflow entering the compressor must remain subsonic (Mach < 1).

Why Is Adjustment Needed?

Aircraft speed changes continuously throughout flight — takeoff, climb, supersonic cruise, landing. The inlet must adapt airflow accordingly.

Common Variable Geometry Devices

Ramps or Wedges — movable components inside the duct that:

  • Change the inlet area
  • Control airflow speed
  • Improve pressure recovery
⚡ Quick Recap
  • Variable geometry = duct changes shape in flight
  • Compressor airflow must always be subsonic (< Mach 1)
  • Uses ramps/wedges to control area, speed, and pressure
06 Inlet Ducts as Diffusers ▾

How the inlet duct converts velocity into pressure.

📖 Definition

A diffuser is a duct section that decreases airflow velocity and increases static pressure.

Airflow PropertyChange in Diffuser
Velocity⬇ Decreases
Static Pressure⬆ Increases
💡 Key Concept

The compressor works best when air enters at lower velocity with increased pressure — this is exactly what the diffuser provides.

⚡ Quick Recap
  • Inlet duct acts as a diffuser
  • Velocity ↓ and Pressure ↑
  • Prepares air for efficient compression
07 Supersonic Inlet Duct Design ▾

How supersonic inlets use convergent-divergent geometry to manage airflow.

💡 Key Concept

At very high speeds, incoming air enters faster than Mach 1. This airflow must be slowed before reaching the compressor.

Convergent Section

  • Duct area decreases
  • Reduces supersonic airflow toward Mach 1

Divergent Section

  • After airflow becomes subsonic, duct area increases
  • Further diffusion occurs — velocity ↓, pressure ↑
Inlet SectionPurpose
ConvergentReduce supersonic speed toward Mach 1
DivergentIncrease pressure and further diffuse airflow
⚡ Quick Recap
  • Supersonic inlet uses convergent–divergent shape
  • Convergent = area ↓ = slows supersonic flow to Mach 1
  • Divergent = area ↑ = further diffusion (subsonic)
08 Methods to Slow Supersonic Airflow ▾

Three methods used (especially in military aircraft) to decelerate supersonic airflow.

Method 1 — Variable Geometry

Movable Components

Uses movable ramps, wedges, or adjustable duct shapes to control airflow speed and pressure.

Method 2 — Airflow Bypass System

Diverting Excess Air

Some inlet air is diverted away from the engine to reduce excessive airflow entering the compressor.

Method 3 — Shock Waves
📖 Definition

A shock wave is a thin region in the airflow where pressure, temperature, density, and velocity change suddenly.

Across a Shock Wave

  • Velocity → decreases
  • Pressure → increases
  • Temperature → increases
  • Density → increases
💡 Key Concept — Combined Systems

Some aircraft combine variable geometry and shock wave systems. The same movable device can change duct area and generate shock waves — improving airflow control, engine efficiency, and supersonic performance.

🧠 Memory Aid — "VBS"

Variable geometry · Bypass system · Shock waves — three methods to slow supersonic airflow.

⚡ Quick Recap
  • Variable geometry: ramps/wedges adjust duct shape
  • Bypass: diverts excess air away from engine
  • Shock waves: sudden pressure/velocity changes slow air
  • Systems can be combined for better control
09 Fan Exhaust Systems in Turbofan Engines ▾

Two ways fan airflow can leave a turbofan engine.

Type 1 — Dual Exhaust Nozzles

  • Fan air exits separately from core exhaust
  • Uses short ducts
  • Core and fan exhaust remain separate

Type 2 — Ducted Fan (Mixed Exhaust)

  • Fan air travels through ducts to the rear
  • Fan air mixes with core exhaust
  • Exits through one common nozzle
Exhaust SystemAirflow Arrangement
Dual Exhaust NozzleSeparate fan and core exhaust
Mixed Exhaust NozzleFan and core airflow combine
⚡ Quick Recap
  • Dual exhaust: fan + core exit separately
  • Mixed exhaust: fan + core combine → single nozzle
