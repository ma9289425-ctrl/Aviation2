Gas turbine engines produce thrust by applying Newton's Laws of Motion, specifically the Law of Momentum.

💡 Key Concept — Newton's Third Law
"For every action, there is an equal and opposite reaction."
The engine accelerates air backward (action) → aircraft is pushed forward (reaction).
↩️

Action

Engine accelerates a mass of air rearward through the exhaust.

✈️

Reaction

Equal and opposite force pushes the aircraft forward — this is thrust.

📐

Law of Momentum

Thrust is proportional to the rate of change of momentum of the air.

Different engine designs use different strategies — small, fast airflow vs. large, slower airflow.

⚡ Turbojet

  • Small mass of air
  • Accelerated to very high velocity
  • High fuel consumption
  • High noise levels

💨 Turbofan

  • Large mass of air
  • Accelerated to lower velocity
  • Better fuel efficiency
  • Quieter operation
Engine Type Airflow Mass Exit Velocity Fuel Efficiency Noise
Turbojet Small Very High Poor High
Turbofan Large Lower Better Lower
⚡ Quick Recap
Turbofan = more air, less speed = more efficient and quieter than turbojet.

Newton's Second Law directly underpins how thrust is generated and what increases it.

Formula — Newton's Second Law
F = m × a
F = Force (Thrust)
m = Mass of air
a = Acceleration
💡 Thrust Increases When...
  • More mass airflow passes through the engine
  • Higher air velocity is produced at the exhaust

Gross thrust is measured at standstill. Net thrust is what matters in flight.

⚓ Gross (Static) Thrust

  • Thrust when aircraft is stationary
  • Typically measured on the ground
  • Inlet air velocity = 0

✈️ Net Thrust

  • Actual usable thrust in flight
  • Gross Thrust minus Ram Drag
  • Depends on inlet vs exhaust velocity difference
Formula — Net Thrust
Net Thrust = Gross Thrust − Ram Drag
💡 Ram Drag Explained
Ram drag is the resistance caused by incoming air entering the engine intake. As airspeed increases, ram drag increases — reducing net thrust relative to gross thrust.
⚠️ Important
As the aircraft accelerates, incoming airflow velocity changes — so thrust characteristics also change. Net thrust is what actually propels the aircraft.

Bernoulli's Principle explains how duct shape affects velocity and pressure — critical for exhaust and compressor design.

💡 Bernoulli's Principle
When fluid velocity increases → pressure decreases. When velocity decreases → pressure increases.

🔻 Convergent Duct

  • Area decreases
  • Velocity increases
  • Pressure decreases
Used in: Exhaust Nozzles

🔺 Divergent Duct

  • Area increases
  • Velocity decreases
  • Pressure increases
Used in: Compressors / Diffusers
Duct TypeAreaVelocityPressureMain Use
ConvergentDecreases ↓Increases ↑Decreases ↓Exhaust nozzle
DivergentIncreases ↑Decreases ↓Increases ↑Compressor/diffuser
⚡ Quick Recap
Convergent = speed up exhaust gas. Divergent = slow down and compress air.
💡 Exhaust Nozzle Types
  • Separate nozzle — fan air exits separately from core exhaust
  • Mixed (common) nozzle — fan air mixes with core exhaust before exiting

Thermal efficiency describes how effectively an engine converts fuel energy into useful work.

💡 Definition — Thermal Efficiency
The ratio of useful work produced to the chemical energy supplied by fuel.

Factors That Improve Thermal Efficiency:

🌡️

Higher TIT

Higher Turbine Inlet Temperature generally increases thermal efficiency.

📦

Higher Compression Ratio

Greater compression before combustion improves efficiency.

⚙️

Component Efficiency

Better compressor and turbine efficiency reduces internal losses.

🔥

Combustion Efficiency

More complete combustion extracts more energy from fuel.

⚠️ Critical Safety Point — Turbine Blade Life
As Turbine Inlet Temperature (TIT) increases → turbine blade life decreases rapidly.
Pilots and engineers must stay within approved temperature limits at all times.
🧠 Operational Note
At constant RPM: higher exhaust temperature usually indicates higher thermal efficiency — but excessive turbine temperature significantly reduces engine service life.

Stagnation density is the key variable linking ambient conditions to engine power output.

💡 Definition — Stagnation Density
Combines the effects of altitude, airspeed, and ambient temperature into a single performance factor.
⚡ Core Principle
Engine power output is proportional to stagnation density.
  • Higher density → improved engine performance
  • Lower density → reduced thrust and power

OAT directly affects air density, which in turn affects mass airflow and thrust.

❄️ Low OAT (Cold Air)

  • Air is denser
  • Greater mass airflow
  • Less compressor work required
  • → Higher thrust output

🌡️ High OAT (Hot Air)

  • Air is less dense
  • Reduced mass airflow
  • More compressor work required
  • → Lower thrust output
🧠 Exam Tip
Cold weather improves turbine engine performance. Hot weather reduces it.
🧠 Memory Aid
C.D.T. Cold air → Dense → more Thrust

As altitude increases, air pressure and density both decrease — reducing engine thrust despite also lower temperatures.

💡 Key Principle
Although temperature also decreases with altitude — the density reduction has a greater effect. Therefore, engine thrust decreases with altitude.
⚠️ Operational Effects at High Altitude Airports
  • Longer takeoff distance required
  • Higher approach speeds needed
  • Reduced climb performance
ConditionEffect on Aircraft
High altitudeLonger takeoff roll
High temperatureReduced climb rate
Low air densityFaster approach speed needed
⚡ Quick Recap
Altitude ↑ → Density ↓ → Thrust ↓ → Performance ↓

Bypass ratio compares the airflow that bypasses the engine core to the airflow through the core.

💡 Definition
Bypass Ratio = Fan Airflow ÷ Core Airflow
Formula — Bypass Ratio
BPR = Fan Airflow / Core Airflow

🚀 Low Bypass Engine

  • Less air bypasses the core
  • Higher exhaust velocity
  • Used in military aircraft

🛫 High Bypass Engine

  • More air bypasses the core
  • Better fuel efficiency
  • Lower noise
  • Common in commercial aircraft
⚡ Quick Recap
High bypass = more bypass air = better efficiency + quieter = commercial aviation standard.

EPR is a key instrument reading that indicates how much power the engine is producing.

💡 Definition — EPR
EPR indicates how much power the engine is producing. A higher EPR = greater engine thrust.
Formula — Engine Pressure Ratio
EPR = Turbine Exhaust Pressure / Compressor Inlet Pressure
⚡ Quick Recap
Higher EPR → greater engine thrust. EPR is used as a power setting indicator in the cockpit.
