01
Introduction to Turbine Lubrication
Why lubrication is essential in gas turbine engines
⌄

Gas turbine engines operate under extreme conditions — very high temperatures, high rotational speeds, and heavy bearing loads. The lubrication system is essential to protect engine components and maintain reliable operation.

Core Functions of Lubrication

🔩
Friction Reduction
Separates moving surfaces to prevent wear on bearings and gears.
🌡️
Cooling
Absorbs and removes heat from bearings and other engine components.
🧹
Cleaning
Carries away contaminants and metallic debris from contact surfaces.
🛡️
Corrosion Protection
Forms a protective film on metal surfaces to prevent oxidation.
⚠️
Safety Critical
Without proper lubrication, bearings may overheat, components may fail, and engine damage can occur.
⚡ Quick Recap
The four main functions of turbine oil are: lubrication, cooling, cleaning, and corrosion protection.
Turbine engines operate at very high temperatures, speeds, and bearing loads.
Bearing cooling is one of the most important functions of the oil system.
02
Types of Lubrication Systems
Wet sump vs. dry sump — key differences and applications
⌄

The two primary design types differ in where the oil is stored. This affects cooling capability, capacity, and performance during aircraft maneuvering.

🪣 Wet Sump
  • Oil stored inside the engine
  • Simpler design
  • Common in smaller engines
  • Limited oil capacity
🛢️ Dry Sump
  • Oil stored in a separate external tank
  • Most common in turbine engines
  • Larger oil capacity
  • Better during maneuvering

Why Dry Sump Is Preferred in Turbines

  • Better cooling capability — larger volume of oil available
  • Larger oil capacity — stored in external tank
  • Improved lubrication control — consistent flow management
  • Better maneuvering performance — oil supply not affected by aircraft attitude
💡
Key Concept
The dry sump system is the most common design in turbine engines. Oil is stored in an external tank, allowing greater capacity and reliable supply during all flight attitudes.
03
Lubrication System Classifications
Systems classified by oil flow method — pressure relief, full flow, total loss
⌄

Beyond where oil is stored, systems are also classified by how oil pressure and flow is managed.

System Type Oil Pressure Application
Pressure Relief Relatively constant Most common system
Full Flow Varies with engine speed Large turbofan engines
Total Loss Oil not recirculated Missiles, target drones
💡
Key Concept
The pressure relief system maintains relatively constant oil pressure and is the most common type. The total loss system does not recirculate oil — it is used only for short-duration engines such as missiles.
⚡ Quick Recap
Pressure relief = most common; maintains constant pressure.
Full flow = pressure varies with speed; used in large turbofans.
Total loss = oil not reused; only for short-duration engines.
04
Main Functions of Turbine Engine Oil
What the oil system does and why bearing cooling is the most critical
⌄

Turbine engine oil performs four primary functions, with cooling being particularly critical due to the proximity of bearings to extremely hot turbine sections.

The Four Primary Functions

  • Lubrication — reduces friction between rotating components
  • Cooling — absorbs and removes heat from bearings
  • Cleaning — carries contaminants away from surfaces
  • Corrosion protection — protective film on metal parts
⚠️
Most Critical Lubrication Point
The turbine bearing area is the most critical lubrication point in the engine. Bearings operate near extremely hot turbine sections and depend heavily on oil cooling.

05
Bearing Cooling Systems
Oil cooling and secondary airflow — how bearings are kept cool
⌄

Turbine bearings are exposed to extreme heat from nearby turbine sections. Two methods are used together: oil cooling and secondary airflow (compressor bleed air).

Method 1 – Oil Cooling

  • Oil circulates around bearings to absorb heat
  • Hot oil is then carried away to the oil cooler

Method 2 – Secondary Airflow

💡
Key Concept – Secondary Airflow
Secondary airflow is compressor bleed air used to cool turbine components. It is taken from early compressor stages — where pressure is adequate but temperature is lower than later stages.

Components Cooled by Secondary Airflow

⚙️
Turbine Bearings
💿
Turbine Disks
🔆
Turbine Blades
🔻
Turbine Vanes
💡
Important Benefit
Air cooling reduces the amount of oil needed for cooling, improving overall system efficiency.
⚡ Quick Recap
Secondary airflow = compressor bleed air used for cooling.
Taken from early compressor stages — lower temperature, adequate pressure.
Cools turbine bearings, disks, blades, and vanes.
Reduces the amount of oil required for cooling.
06
Oil Coolers
Air oil coolers and fuel oil coolers — types, function, and operation
⌄

Because turbine oil absorbs large amounts of heat from bearings, oil coolers are necessary to reduce oil temperature before recirculation.

Cooler Type Cooling Medium Notes
Air Oil Cooler Airflow Installed at forward end of engine; used in dry sump systems
Fuel Oil Cooler Fuel (heat exchanger) Cools oil AND heats fuel to prevent ice formation

Fuel Oil Cooler – How It Works

1
Hot oil enters the heat exchanger

Oil from the scavenge system passes through the fuel oil cooler.

2
Heat transfers from oil to fuel

The oil transfers its heat to the fuel flowing through the exchanger.

3
Oil is cooled — fuel is warmed

Cooled oil returns to the system; warmer fuel proceeds to combustion.

💡
Dual Benefit
The fuel oil cooler provides two benefits simultaneously: it cools the engine oil and heats the fuel to help prevent ice formation before combustion.
07
Thermostatic Bypass Valves
Temperature control and protection against cooler failure
⌄

The thermostatic bypass valve controls oil temperature by regulating how much oil passes through the cooler, and protects the system if the cooler becomes blocked.

Normal Operation

  • Valve controls the amount of oil flowing through the cooler
  • Maintains proper oil temperature at all times

Bypass Operation

  • If the cooler is blocked, damaged, or restricted, the valve opens automatically
  • Oil bypasses the cooler and continues to circulate
⚠️
Why This Is Critical
Without the bypass function, a blocked cooler could cause excessive pressure buildup or oil starvation — both potentially catastrophic for the engine.
💡
Key Concept
The thermostatic bypass valve serves two roles: temperature regulation under normal conditions, and cooler protection under fault conditions.
08
Check Valves
Preventing reverse oil flow and its dangerous consequences
⌄

Check valves prevent oil from draining back into the engine by gravity after shutdown. Without them, oil can accumulate in unwanted areas and create serious hazards.

Purpose

  • Prevent reverse oil flow after engine shutdown
  • Stop oil draining into low-lying engine areas by gravity

Consequences Without Check Valves

⚠️
Safety Critical – Without Check Valves
Oil may accumulate → leading to:
· Internal oil fires
· Gearbox flooding
· Cabin air contamination

Design Details

  • Most check valves are spring-loaded ball-and-seat type
  • Typical opening pressure: 2 to 5 psi
💡
Key Concept
Check valves are especially important in dry sump systems, where the external tank and pipework create more opportunity for reverse oil flow under gravity.
09
Deoiler
Centrifugal separation of oil from breather air
⌄

The deoiler prevents oil loss by separating oil droplets from the breather air before it is vented overboard. It uses centrifugal action.

Operating Principle

1
Breather air (containing oil mist) enters the deoiler

Air from the engine venting system passes into a rotating impeller.

2
Spinning impeller causes centrifugal separation

The spinning action flings heavier oil droplets outward. Lighter air remains near the center.

3
Oil and air are separated

Oil is returned to the sump/tank. Clean air is vented overboard.

SubstanceDestination
OilReturned to sump / tank
AirVented overboard
💡
Key Concept
The deoiler uses centrifugal force via a rotating impeller to separate oil from breather air, preventing oil loss and environmental contamination.
10
Magnetic Chip Detectors
Early warning of internal engine damage via metallic particle detection
⌄

Magnetic chip detectors are a key engine health monitoring tool. They attract ferrous metal particles circulating in the oil, providing early warning of internal damage.

Purpose and Function

  • Detect ferrous (magnetic) metallic particles in the oil
  • Metal particles indicate bearing wear, gear damage, or engine failure
  • Provide early warning before catastrophic damage occurs

Typical Installation Locations

🔗
Scavenge Lines
Most common location — captures particles from returning oil.
🪣
Oil Sumps
Detects particles settling in the sump.
🛢️
Oil Tanks
Secondary detection point in the storage tank.

Maintenance Procedure

1
Detector is removed

Removed from scavenge line or sump during scheduled inspection.

2
Checked for metal particles

Visual and tactile inspection for ferrous debris on the magnetic tip.

3
If normal: cleaned and reinstalled

No action required if only minor clean metal is found.

!
If significant metal found: investigate immediately

Source must be identified before returning the engine to service.

⚠️
Safety Critical
Metal particles on a chip detector may indicate bearing wear, gear damage, or internal engine failure. The source must be investigated immediately.
📝
Exam Tip
Magnetic chip detectors provide early warning of internal engine damage — a key exam point. Remember they detect ferrous particles only.
⚡ Quick Recap – Full System
Turbine oil: lubricates, cools, cleans, and protects against corrosion.
Dry sump is the most common turbine engine system — oil in external tank.
Secondary airflow = compressor bleed air from early stages — cools turbine components.
Air oil coolers use airflow; fuel oil coolers use fuel as a heat exchange medium.
Thermostatic bypass valve controls temperature and bypasses cooler if blocked.
Check valves prevent reverse oil flow after shutdown — prevent fires and flooding.
Deoiler uses centrifugal action to recover oil from breather air.
Chip detectors attract ferrous particles — early warning of engine damage.
