Purpose of Bearings

Why turbine engines need bearings and what drives the quantity required

💡
Core Function Bearings support the main engine rotor, maintain shaft alignment, and enable smooth, continuous rotation under demanding conditions.
What determines how many bearings are needed?
Primary Factors
Rotor Characteristics
  • Rotor length
  • Rotor weight
Design Factor
Compressor Type
  • Multi-spool designs require more bearing support
  • A two-spool compressor increases bearing count
🔩

Types of Bearings Used

Minimum arrangement per shaft and when additional support is required

✅
Minimum Arrangement — Per Shaft Each turbine engine shaft typically requires a minimum of one ball bearing and one roller bearing.
Ball Bearing
Radial + Axial Loads
  • Handles both radial and axial (thrust) loads
  • Positioned to absorb thrust
Roller Bearing
Radial Loads Only
  • Handles radial loads only
  • Larger contact area — better load distribution
⚠️
Additional Roller Bearings May Be Required When: The shaft is long or subject to significant vibration.
⚖️

Ball vs Roller — Comparison

Load handling capabilities — a critical exam distinction

Feature 🔵 Ball Bearing 🟠 Roller Bearing
Radial Load ✓ Yes ✓ Yes
Axial (Thrust) Load ✓ Yes ✗ No
Contact Area Point contact Line contact — larger area
Primary Role Thrust & radial support Radial support only
🧠
Memory Aid — "BALL does ALL" Ball bearings handle all load directions: both radial and axial.
Roller bearings handle only radial — think "rolls along a surface."
🔄

Antifriction Bearings

Why they are the preferred choice — advantages and critical limitations

💡
Definition Bearings designed to reduce friction between moving parts through rolling elements (balls or rollers) rather than sliding contact.
Advantages
  • Low rotational resistance
  • Accurate alignment of rotating parts
  • Cost-effective
  • Easy to replace
  • Can handle short-term overloads
  • Simple to cool, lubricate, and maintain
  • Support both radial and axial loads
  • Good resistance to high temperatures
Advantages Summary
Why They Are Preferred
  • Low friction resistance
  • Accurate alignment
  • Easy maintenance
  • Handle radial + axial loads
⚠️ Disadvantages
Critical Limitations
  • Vulnerable to foreign matter
  • Tend to fail without appreciable warning
🚨
Safety-Critical Note Antifriction bearings may fail without appreciable warning. This makes monitoring, filtration, and scheduled inspection essential.

Bearing Mounting & Support

Housing design, shaft journals, securing methods, and self-aligning bearings

Housing
Bearing Housing
  • Must be strong
  • Must support all rotor loads
Shaft Interface
Journal
  • Bearing runs on a machined journal on the shaft
  • Precision surface for smooth contact
Retention
Securing Method
  • Held using snap rings
  • Or other locking devices
Self-Aligning Bearings
🔄
Self-Aligning Design Mounted in a spherical seat, these bearings allow slight shaft movement without inducing stress — important for accommodating thermal expansion and shaft deflection.
🔒

Seals — Overview & Location

Why seals are essential and where they are installed

💡
Purpose of Seals Prevent oil leakage and ensure proper lubrication delivery to bearings and rotating components.
Location & Design
  • Seals are installed in the bearing housing
  • The shaft provides a matching surface called "Lands"
  • Lands fit closely with seals to reduce oil leakage
Location
Bearing Housing
  • Seals sit within the bearing housing
Shaft Interface
"Lands" Surface
  • Matching surface on shaft
  • Close fit reduces oil leakage
🌀

Types of Oil Seals

Labyrinth, helical, and their working principles — exam favourite

🟡 Type 1
Labyrinth Seal
  • Uses pressurized air
  • Minimizes leakage through controlled pathways
  • Method: Pressure-based
🟢 Type 2
Helical (Thread) Seal
  • Uses reverse threading
  • Directs oil back inward to prevent leakage
  • Method: Mechanical redirection
Side-by-Side Comparison
Feature 🟡 Labyrinth Seal 🟢 Helical Seal
Working Principle Air pressure Reverse thread
Leakage Control Pressure-based Mechanical redirection
🧠
Memory Aid Labyrinth = air Lock (pressure pathway).
Helical = reverse Handed thread redirects oil inward.
⬛

Carbon Seals

Modern high-performance seals used in turbine engines

Material
Carbon Construction
  • Made from carbon material
  • Used in modern engines
Mechanism
Spring-Loaded Design
  • Spring-loaded — similar to carbon brushes in electric motors
  • Presses against a surface to create a tight seal
⚠️
What Carbon Seals Prevent They stop oil from escaping into the compressor airflow or the turbine section — both of which would create serious operational issues.
⚡ Quick Recap — All Seal Types
  • Labyrinth: uses pressurized air through controlled pathways
  • Helical: uses reverse threading to redirect oil inward
  • Carbon: spring-loaded, presses against a surface for a tight seal

Quick Recap — Exam Focus

Everything you must know, at a glance

⚡ Bearings
  • Bearings support and stabilize the rotor system
  • Ball bearings → radial + axial loads
  • Roller bearings → radial loads only
  • Minimum per shaft: 1 ball + 1 roller
  • Bearing count driven by: rotor length & weight
  • Antifriction bearings: preferred for efficiency — but fail without warning
  • Self-aligning bearings sit in a spherical seat
⚡ Seals
  • Seals prevent oil leakage and maintain system efficiency
  • Labyrinth: pressure-based (air)
  • Helical: mechanical redirection (reverse thread)
  • Carbon: spring-loaded, modern, high-performance
  • Seal surface on shaft = "Lands"
