01 — Energy Overview
The foundation of all aircraft motion and engine operation
💡
Key Concept
Energy is the ability to perform work. In aviation, energy enables aircraft to move, accelerate, produce thrust, and generate lift.
Two Main Types of Energy
Potential Energy
Stored / at rest
e.g. Fuel in tanks, aircraft on jacks
Kinetic Energy
Energy in motion
e.g. Aircraft rolling on runway
⚡
Quick Recap
Energy = ability to do work  |  Two types: Potential (stored) & Kinetic (motion)
🔋
02 — Potential Energy
Stored energy — position, elasticity, or chemical form
Three Forms of Potential Energy
1. Position
Height / Location
Water in elevated tank · Aircraft on jacks
2. Elastic Distortion
Stretched / Compressed
Compressed spring · Bungee cord
3. Chemical
Chemical reaction
Aviation fuel · Aircraft batteries
PE = W × H
PE = Potential Energy
W = Weight
H = Height
⚠️
Exam Watch
Aviation fuel is a form of chemical potential energy — it stores energy that is released through combustion, not motion.
⚡
Quick Recap
Potential energy is stored. Three forms: position, elasticity, chemical. Formula: PE = W × H
🚀
03 — Kinetic Energy
Energy of motion — depends on mass and velocity
💡
Definition
Kinetic energy is energy possessed by a body due to its motion.
KE = ½mv²
m = Mass
v = Velocity
Aviation Examples
  • Aircraft taking off along a runway
  • Rotating turbine blades inside an engine
  • Spinning flywheel
⚠️
Critical Point
Velocity is squared in the formula. Doubling speed increases kinetic energy by four times — critical for understanding stopping distances.
⚡
Quick Recap
KE = energy of motion. Formula: KE = ½mv². Greater speed = exponentially greater KE.
💪
04 — Force
The push or pull that drives all aircraft motion
💡
Definition
Force is a push or pull that tends to move an object. Without force: no motion, no work, no thrust.
F = m × a
F = Force
m = Mass
a = Acceleration
Aviation Applications of F = ma
  • Calculating engine thrust
  • Determining aircraft acceleration on takeoff roll
  • Analysing engine performance
⚡
Quick Recap
Force = push or pull. Formula: F = ma. Foundation of Newton's Second Law & thrust calculations.
🔧
05 — Work & Torque
Linear vs rotational force — a critical distinction
Work
Force applied over a linear distance.
W = F × d
⚠️
If the object does not move, no mechanical work is done — even if force is applied.
Torque
A twisting force that causes rotation.
τ = F × d
  • Turning a wrench
  • Rotating propeller shaft
  • Turbine shaft rotation
⚠️
Exam Distinction — Do Not Confuse
Work and Torque use the same formula (Force × Distance) but describe different phenomena:
 • Work → linear movement  |  Torque → rotational movement
⚡
Quick Recap
Work = force × linear distance. Torque = twisting force × distance. Same formula — different physical actions.

06 — Motion, Speed, Velocity & Acceleration
Understanding how objects move and change speed
Motion & Kinematics
  • Motion — continuous change in position relative to another object.
  • Kinematics — studies velocity, acceleration & distance without analysing the forces causing motion.
Speed
s = d / t
  • Magnitude only — no direction
  • Scalar quantity
Velocity
Speed with direction
  • Example: 250 knots eastward
  • Vector quantity
⚠️
Key Distinction — Speed vs Velocity
Speed tells you how fast. Velocity tells you how fast and in which direction. Velocity is a vector; speed is a scalar.
Acceleration
Rate of change of velocity over time.
a = Δv / t
Δv = Change in velocity
t = Time
⚡
Quick Recap
Speed = magnitude only · Velocity = speed + direction (vector) · Acceleration = rate of velocity change: a = Δv/t

07 — Newton's Laws of Motion
The three fundamental laws governing all aircraft movement
Newton's First Law
Law of Inertia
"An object at rest stays at rest, and an object in motion stays in motion, unless acted upon by an external force."
  • Inertia — the tendency of matter to resist changes in motion.
  • Aviation example: passengers lurch forward when an aircraft stops suddenly.
Newton's Second Law
F = ma
"Force causes acceleration — proportional to mass and in the same direction as the applied force."
F = m × a
Used to calculate thrust & aircraft acceleration
Newton's Third Law
Action & Reaction
"For every action, there is an equal and opposite reaction."
  • Explains jet thrust — gases expelled backward → aircraft moves forward.
  • Also explains rocket propulsion & propeller action.
3×N
1st Law: Inertia — resist change
2nd Law: F = ma — force causes acceleration
3rd Law: Action = opposite Reaction (explains jet thrust)
⚡
Quick Recap
1st: Inertia (resist change) · 2nd: F = ma · 3rd: Action = Reaction (jet thrust). All three laws directly apply to aircraft operation.

08 — Bernoulli's Principle
The velocity-pressure relationship that explains wing lift
💡
Core Principle
As the velocity of a fluid increases, its static pressure decreases — and vice versa.
Venturi Tube — How it Works

A Venturi has a converging section → narrow throat → diverging section.

Wide Area
Velocity: Low
Pressure: High
Narrow Throat
Velocity: High
Pressure: Low
Wide Area
Velocity: Low
Pressure: High
Aviation Applications
  • Wing lift — faster airflow over upper surface reduces pressure → net upward force
  • Carburettors — venturi effect draws fuel into airflow
  • Turbine engine inlet ducts — airflow management
⚡
Quick Recap
↑ Velocity → ↓ Pressure. The venturi effect underpins wing lift, carburettors, and engine inlet ducts.

09 — Gas Laws
Boyle's & Charles' Laws — engine compression and airflow
Boyle's Law
At constant temperature — pressure and volume are inversely proportional.
  • ↑ Pressure → ↓ Volume
  • ↓ Pressure → ↑ Volume
Aviation Uses
Engine compression · Pneumatic systems · Gas turbine operation
Charles' Law
At constant pressure — temperature and volume are directly proportional.
  • ↑ Temperature → ↑ Volume
Aviation Uses
Expansion of heated air · Turbine engine airflow behaviour
B↔C
Boyle: Pressure ↕ Volume (inverse) — constant Temperature
Charles: Temperature ↕ Volume (direct) — constant Pressure
⚡
Quick Recap
Boyle: P↑ V↓ (constant T) — Charles: T↑ V↑ (constant P). Both laws govern engine compression & gas expansion in turbines.

10 — Thrust & Propulsion
How engines produce the forward force to fly
💡
Definition
Thrust is the forward propulsive force produced by an engine, used to overcome drag, accelerate, and maintain flight.
How a Jet Engine Produces Thrust
  • Step 1: Air is drawn into the engine
  • Step 2: Air is compressed
  • Step 3: Fuel is added and burned
  • Step 4: Hot gases are expelled rearward at high velocity
  • Result: Aircraft moves forward — Newton's 3rd Law
Exhaust Nozzles
Convergent Nozzle
↑ Exhaust velocity
Produces maximum subsonic thrust
Choked Nozzle
Gases reach speed of sound
Maximum efficiency for subsonic operation
Propulsion Types Compared
Engine TypeMethodKey Feature
Turbojet Accelerates small air mass High exhaust velocity
Turboprop Accelerates large air mass Lower velocity, via propeller
Rocket Carries own fuel & oxidizer Can operate in space — no atmosphere needed
⚠️
Rockets vs Aircraft Engines
Aircraft engines require atmospheric oxygen for combustion. Rockets carry their own fuel + oxidizer — this is why rockets can operate in space.
⚡
Quick Recap
Thrust = forward force via Newton's 3rd Law. Jet engines: intake → compress → burn → expel. Rockets carry own oxidizer (space-capable).

Key Definitions — Quick Reference
All must-know terms in one place
Force
A push or pull that tends to move an object. F = ma.
Work
Done when a force moves an object through a distance. W = F × d. No movement = no work.
Torque
A twisting force causing rotation. τ = F × d. Different from work despite same formula.
Energy
The ability to perform work. Exists as potential (stored) or kinetic (motion).
Velocity
Speed with direction. A vector quantity. e.g. 250 knots eastward.
Acceleration
Rate of change of velocity. a = Δv / t.
Thrust
The forward propulsive force produced by an engine to overcome drag and maintain flight.
Inertia
The tendency of matter to resist changes in its state of motion (Newton's 1st Law).
Power
The rate at which work is done. 1 hp = 550 ft·lb/s = 746 W.
