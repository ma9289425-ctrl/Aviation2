Introduction to the Compressor Section

Understanding the core function of compressors

▼
💡 Primary Function

The compressor section supplies compressed air to the combustion chamber and increases air pressure before combustion.

Why Compression Is Necessary

Compressed air enables:

  • Efficient fuel combustion
  • Higher engine efficiency
  • Greater thrust and power output
⚡ Quick Recap
  • Compressor supplies air to combustion chamber
  • Increases pressure for efficient combustion
  • Also provides bleed air for aircraft/engine systems
💨

Bleed Air System

Secondary uses of compressed air

▼
💡 Definition

Bleed air is high-pressure compressed air extracted from different compressor stages through small openings called bleed ports.

Stage Selection

Stage Pressure Temperature
Earlier stages Lower Lower
Later stages Higher Higher

Common Uses of Bleed Air

🌡️ Cabin Pressurization

Provides breathable pressurized air for passengers and crew

❄️ Heating & Cooling

Used in air conditioning systems

🧊 De/Anti-Icing

Hot bleed air prevents ice on wings, engine inlets, other surfaces

🔄 Engine Starting

Pneumatic engine starting of turbine engines

🧠 Exam Tip

Bleed air is usually taken from high-pressure compressor stages because pressure and temperature are greatest there.

Bleed Air Control

Flow is controlled by bleed valves (automatic or manual) that open or close airflow into the pneumatic system.

⚡ Quick Recap
  • Bleed air = compressed air from compressor stages
  • Uses: pressurization, heating/cooling, anti-icing, engine starting
  • Later stages = higher pressure & temperature
  • Bleed valves control the flow
⚙️

Compressor Types Overview

Two main types used in gas turbine engines

▼
Compressor Type Main Use
Centrifugal Flow Turboprop and turboshaft engines
Axial Flow Turbofan engines
⚡ Quick Recap
  • Centrifugal = air moves outward (smaller engines)
  • Axial = air moves parallel to engine axis (turbofans)
🌀

Centrifugal Flow Compressor

Air accelerated outward by centrifugal force

▼
💡 Operating Principle

The centrifugal compressor accelerates air outward using centrifugal force.

Main Components

1. Impeller (Rotor)

Rotating component that accelerates air outward

2. Diffuser (Stator)

Slows the air and converts velocity into pressure

3. Compressor Manifold

Collects compressed air and directs it to combustion chambers

🧠 Key Value

Pressure ratio per stage: approximately 8:1

✔️ Advantages
  • High pressure increase per stage
  • Simple and robust design
  • Good for smaller engines
✘ Limitations
  • Larger frontal area
  • Efficiency decreases with many stages
  • Usually limited to 1-2 stages
⚡ Quick Recap
  • Air moves outward (centrifugal force)
  • Components: Impeller → Diffuser → Manifold
  • High pressure per stage (~8:1)
  • Best for smaller engines (turboprop/turboshaft)
➡️

Axial Flow Compressor

Air flows parallel to engine axis

▼
💡 Operating Principle

The axial compressor compresses air while it flows parallel to the engine axis.

Main Components

Component Function
Rotor Rotating blades on shaft — accelerate air rearward
Stator Stationary vanes — convert velocity into pressure, direct airflow

Compression Process

Each stage = one rotor row + one stator row = one pressure stage

Rotor function: Adds velocity energy to airflow

Stator function: Reduces velocity, increases pressure, directs airflow to next stage

🧠 Key Values
  • Pressure ratio per stage: approximately 1.25:1
  • Modern engines may use 16+ stages
⚡ Quick Recap
  • Air flows parallel to engine axis
  • Rotor accelerates, Stator converts velocity to pressure
  • One stage = Rotor + Stator (~1.25:1 ratio)
  • More stages = higher overall pressure ratio
  • Used in most turbofan engines

Compressor Blade Construction

Materials and attachment methods

▼

Blade Materials

  • Stainless steel — common for rotor blades
  • Titanium — especially rear stages

Blade Attachment Methods

  • Bulb type
  • Fir-tree type
💡 Blade Profiles

Blade tips are often thinned using blade profiles (cutouts) to reduce severe damage if blades contact the casing.

⚡ Quick Recap
  • Materials: Stainless steel, Titanium
  • Attachments: Bulb type, Fir-tree type
  • Thinned tips reduce casing contact damage

Inlet Guide Vanes (IGV)

Directing airflow into the first rotor stage

▼
💡 Purpose

Inlet Guide Vanes direct airflow into the first rotor stage and provide proper airflow angle ("whirl").

Variable Inlet Guide Vanes (VIGV)

VIGVs automatically adjust angle based on:

  • RPM
  • Ambient conditions

Purpose: Ensure airflow enters rotor at the most efficient angle of attack.

⚡ Quick Recap
  • IGV = directs air into first rotor
  • VIGV = variable, adjusts based on RPM & conditions
  • Goal: optimal angle of attack
🔄

Variable Stator Vanes (VSV)

Adjusting airflow between compressor stages

▼
💡 Function

Variable Stator Vanes adjust airflow angle between compressor stages for optimized performance.

VIGV vs VSV

System Position
VIGV Before rotor blades (at inlet)
VSV Behind rotor blades (between stages)
🧠 Control System

Modern VSV systems are often controlled by FADEC (Full Authority Digital Engine Control).

⚡ Quick Recap
  • VSV adjusts airflow between stages
  • VIGV = before rotor, VSV = behind rotor
  • Often FADEC-controlled
⚠️

Compressor Stall & Surge

Critical safety concepts for exam

▼
💡 Blades as Airfoils

Compressor blades act like aircraft wings — they can stall if angle of attack becomes excessive.

⚠️ Critical Definitions

Compressor Stall: One or more compressor blades lose smooth airflow (local disruption).

Compressor Surge: The entire compressor stalls (complete instability).

Stall vs Surge

Condition Description
Stall Local airflow disruption
Surge Complete compressor instability
⚠️ Effects of Stall & Surge
  • Loud bangs
  • Vibrations
  • Flame from exhaust
  • Loss of thrust
  • Engine damage
🧠 Remember

Surge is more severe than stall — it affects the entire compressor.

⚡ Quick Recap
  • Blades act like wings — can stall
  • Stall = local disruption
  • Surge = complete compressor stall (more severe)
  • Symptoms: bangs, vibration, flame, thrust loss
🎛️

Airflow Control Systems

Preventing stall and surge

▼
💡 Purpose

Airflow control systems prevent stall and surge while maintaining efficient compressor operation over a wide RPM range.

Main Control Components

1. Bleed Valves

Release excess air to reduce back pressure

2. VIGV

Variable Inlet Guide Vanes — adjust inlet airflow angle

3. VSV

Variable Stator Vanes — optimize inter-stage airflow

Bleed Valves & Stall Prevention

Problem: High combustion chamber back pressure can cause compressor instability.

Solution: Bleed valves release excess compressor air → reduces rear-stage pressure → prevents front-stage stall.

🧠 Bleed Valve Operation
  • Open at idle RPM
  • Open during rapid acceleration
  • Closed during cruise RPM (airflow more stable)
⚡ Quick Recap
  • Three main systems: Bleed valves, VIGV, VSV
  • Bleed valves release excess air to reduce back pressure
  • Bleed valves: Open at idle/acceleration, Closed at cruise
  • VIGV/VSV improve efficiency and reduce stall risk
